\chapter{Background and Related Work}
\label{chap:background}

This chapter reviews the prior art that informs our design. We start with closed-loop payment systems and the transit and festival deployments that have shaped the field, then look at EMV's offline authorisation as the canonical open-loop counter-example, and finally summarise the architectural patterns and distributed-systems theory that the design choices in Chapter~\ref{chap:architecture} rest on. The arguments \emph{for} our specific architecture live in later chapters; here we describe what already exists.

\section{Closed-Loop Payment Systems}
\label{sec:closed-loop}

A closed-loop payment system, as described by SDK.finance~\cite{SDKFinance2024}, ``confines transactions to a specific network or ecosystem.'' The same operator is the issuer and the acquirer, so the money stays inside the operator's perimeter from top-up to refund. This is the opposite of an open-loop system like Visa or Mastercard, where the transaction crosses banks, card schemes, and processors that the merchant does not control.

Festivals lean toward closed-loop for four reasons. \textbf{Connectivity.} A festival is already a place where the network has a bad time, so depending on external authorisation hosts on the other side of the world is asking for trouble. With a closed loop, all authorisation logic lives inside the operator's own infrastructure. \textbf{Fees.} Open-loop processing eats interchange, scheme, and processor fees per transaction; in a closed loop, the only point where external fees apply is when money enters the system through a top-up, and operators usually negotiate lower rates with a strategic provider. \textbf{Regulation.} In the EU, electronic money is a regulated activity~\cite{EMD2009}, but festival systems often fit under the limited-network exemption of PSD2~\cite{PSD2015} by issuing prepaid tokens redeemable only inside the venue. Showing balances as ``tokens'' rather than euros reinforces this framing. \textbf{Control.} The operator decides transaction limits, refund policies, vendor approvals, and settlement timing without a card scheme telling them what is allowed.

The one moment a closed-loop system has to talk to the outside world is when real money enters the system. This requires an active connection to an external provider like Stripe and is therefore tied to the network being up.

\subsection{Transit Card Systems}
\label{subsec:transit-cards}

Transit cards (Suica in Tokyo, Oyster in London, Octopus in Hong Kong) are the most successful example of closed-loop payments that work without constant connectivity. Shirakawa~\cite{Shirakawa2002} describes how Suica's FeliCa chip~\cite{SonyFeliCa} handles fare deduction locally at the gate through encrypted read-write operations, completing each transaction in roughly 0.1 seconds without a network round-trip. Mutual authentication between the chip and the gate generates a fresh session key per transaction, which keeps cloning hard: the chip's secret key never leaves its secure memory, so captured radio traffic is useless on the next tap. Newer FeliCa generations use AES and the most recent revision is certified at Common Criteria EAL6+, which covers resistance to physical and side-channel attacks. Logs are uploaded from gates to central servers in batches for reconciliation and reporting, so these systems are not purely offline; they are offline at the point of sale and online for the back office.

Octopus~\cite{SonyOctopus, Chau2003} adopted FeliCa four years before Suica and grew from a transit card into a general-purpose stored-value card accepted at convenience stores, restaurants, and parking. By the early 2000s it had over 11 million cards in a city of 7 million, which shows that the closed-loop, card-stored-balance pattern scales beyond its original domain.

The relevant takeaways for a festival system are: sub-second transactions are a hard requirement when crowds queue, an on-chip balance is what makes that possible, and the controlled environment of a transit network (fixed terminals, private back-office network, predictable usage) does not transfer directly to a temporary venue with mobile vendor terminals.

\subsection{Festival Payment Systems}
\label{subsec:campus-cards}

Cashless festivals have been deployed by Tappit, Intellitix, Weezevent, and Glownet at hundreds of events worldwide. The typical setup hands out an RFID wristband at the gate, lets attendees load credits through cash stations or an online portal, and processes payments at vendor terminals that read the wristband, deduct the amount, and write the new balance back. Industry data~\cite{Tappit2024} reports notable speed and revenue gains versus cash, and academic work on cashless payment behaviour~\cite{Schomburgk2024} confirms a small but statistically significant positive effect of cashless on consumer spending.

Two limitations of the wristband-only model are widely documented. First, the chips most commonly used are MIFARE Classic, whose proprietary CRYPTO1 cipher was broken in practice by Garcia et al.~\cite{Garcia2008}; more modern DESFire chips address this but are more expensive, so older deployments still rely on the weaker hardware. Second, the architectures of these commercial systems are proprietary and essentially undocumented in academic literature. The failures that are public, such as the Download Festival 2015 crash mentioned in Chapter~\ref{chap:introduction}, are reconstructed from press reports rather than from any published post-mortem.

Conversations with people involved in payment infrastructure at Untold (one of the largest festivals in Romania) added a piece of practical context: even with on-site servers and a private network, top-up sync delays of two to four hours across terminals were common when Wi-Fi coverage degraded. Reliable Wi-Fi at venue scale is itself a hard infrastructure problem, not just a deployment detail.

\section{Offline authorization in open-loop systems}
\label{subsec:emv}

EMV (named after Europay, Mastercard, and Visa) is the canonical open-loop card standard. It is not closed-loop, but it is the most widely deployed example of offline authorisation, and it is worth understanding because it solves a similar problem under stricter constraints. EMV cards carry a set of risk parameters: a per-card offline floor, a maximum number of consecutive offline transactions, and a cumulative offline amount. When the merchant terminal cannot reach the issuer's host, it authorises locally if all three checks pass and forces an online attempt otherwise.

The design distributes risk: the issuer decides how much offline exposure a given cardholder is allowed, and the merchant terminal can have its own floor below which it always accepts. The known weakness, demonstrated by Murdoch et al.~\cite{Murdoch2010} as a man-in-the-middle attack on EMV's PIN verification, is the broader point that any system whose authorisation depends on a device in the user's possession is vulnerable if that device is compromised.

\section{Offline-First Architecture Patterns}
\label{sec:offline-first}

Building a system that has to work without a network puts it somewhere on a spectrum from fully online (every operation needs a round-trip) to fully offline (the network is only used to sync logs in the background). Real systems sit in between. This section reviews four architectural patterns from the literature that are relevant to systems on that spectrum.

\subsection{Local-First Software}
\label{subsec:local-first}

The term ``local-first software'' was introduced by Kleppmann et al.~\cite{Kleppmann2019}. The principle is that the primary copy of the data lives on the user's device, and the cloud syncs between devices instead of being the source of truth. The seven ideals listed in the paper (no spinners, multi-device, optional network, seamless collaboration, longevity, security, ownership) define a target for collaboration apps.

The pattern fits document-style data, where two divergent versions can be merged at any time without losing meaning. It does not fit balances directly, because money is a conserved quantity rather than a piece of text: if two replicas of a balance both authorise a withdrawal, the merge cannot simply union them. The argument for treating local-first as a partial fit, as a read cache rather than as the primary copy, belongs in Chapter~\ref{chap:architecture}; here we record only what the principle says.

\subsection{Conflict-Free Replicated Data Types}
\label{subsec:crdts}

CRDTs, formalised by Shapiro et al.~\cite{Shapiro2011}, are data structures whose operations are commutative, associative, and idempotent, so replicas converge regardless of the order updates arrive in. The simplest example is a G-Counter (grow-only counter), and PN-Counters extend the idea to allow decrements through paired increments.

CRDTs guarantee convergence but not invariants. Kleppmann's later work on JSON CRDTs~\cite{Kleppmann2017json} notes that the converged state is not necessarily one any single replica would have accepted on its own. For a payment system that has to enforce ``balance >= 0'', this is the relevant limitation: the convergence story is solved, but the constraint enforcement is not.

\subsection{Event Sourcing and CQRS}
\label{subsec:event-sourcing}

Event sourcing, as described by Fowler~\cite{Fowler2005}, stores the sequence of events that produced the current state instead of the state itself. The current state is reconstructed by replaying events, which gives a complete audit trail by construction. CQRS (Command Query Responsibility Segregation), described by Young~\cite{Young2010}, separates the write side of a system (commands that produce events) from the read side (queries against a denormalised projection). The two patterns are often used together, with events as the durable record and the read model derived from them.

\subsection{Edge Computing}
\label{subsec:edge-computing}

Edge computing, as framed by Shi et al.~\cite{Shi2016}, moves computation closer to where data is produced rather than centralising it. In a festival the equivalent is the vendor terminal: it can read the chip, validate, and record locally, only involving the cloud when the network is reachable. Trade-off: edge nodes work with potentially stale data; how stale is acceptable, and how to bound the resulting risk, is a design choice.

\section{Conflict Resolution in Distributed Systems}
\label{sec:conflict-resolution}

When multiple terminals act independently they will end up with different views. Distributed systems have a long literature on what guarantees are achievable in this setting and what concepts are useful for thinking about it.

\subsection{The CAP Theorem}
\label{subsec:cap-theorem}

Brewer's conjecture, formalised by Gilbert and Lynch~\cite{Gilbert2002}, says a distributed system cannot simultaneously guarantee consistency, availability, and partition tolerance: when a partition happens, the system has to pick one of the first two. Network partitions in a festival environment are a regular occurrence, not an edge case, so the partition leg of CAP is not optional.

\subsection{Eventual Consistency}
\label{subsec:eventual-consistency}

Vogels~\cite{Vogels2009} defines eventual consistency as the guarantee that, if no new updates are made, all replicas eventually converge. The word ``eventually'' is doing a lot of work; in practice convergence happens quickly once partitions heal. The canonical real-world example is Amazon's Dynamo~\cite{DeCandia2007}, which accepts writes on any reachable node and resolves conflicting versions with application-specific logic when partitions heal.

\subsection{Transaction Ordering}
\label{subsec:transaction-ordering}

Distributed systems theory offers logical and vector clocks for tracking causal ordering between events on different nodes when physical clocks disagree~\cite{DeCandia2007}. These tools are necessary when concurrent writers can target the same object and the system needs to know which write happened first. Whether they are needed in any given system depends on whether such concurrency exists, not on the absolute scale of the workload.

For festival transactions, the realistic granularity matters: transactions happen at human speed (a person walks to a vendor and taps), and even cheap NTP keeps wall-clocks within a few hundred milliseconds of each other, which is far below the spacing between two transactions involving the same wristband. Whether wall-clock timestamps are sufficient as an ordering mechanism in a specific architecture is a design question we revisit in Chapter~\ref{chap:architecture}.
