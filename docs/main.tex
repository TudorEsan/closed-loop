ABSTRACT
\vspace{0.5cm}
\hrule
\vspace{0.5cm}
%\cleardoublepage

This thesis presents the design and implementation of a cloud-based, closed-loop payment system for music festivals and large outdoor events, featuring a modular offline extension for degraded connectivity scenarios. While festival environments are known to suffer from network instability due to mass gatherings, we argue that a fully offline-first architecture introduces unnecessary complexity for the majority of use cases. Instead, we propose an online-default system with real-time transaction processing and centralized wallet management, augmented by a lightweight offline module that can be easily deployed when network conditions require it. The offline extension leverages local balance snapshots, transaction queuing, and batch synchronization to maintain payment availability during connectivity loss, while keeping the core architecture simple and scalable. We explore the challenges that arise at the boundary between online and offline operation particularly regarding balance consistency, double-spend prevention during offline windows, and conflict reconciliation upon reconnection. Also we will be evaluating strategies for minimizing their impact. The proposed architecture is assessed against the specific requirements of festival environments, demonstrating that treating offline capability as a modular add-on rather than a foundational design principle results in a more practical and maintainable system.
